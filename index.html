Personal Story

My path toward advocacy began long before I had the language to describe it. Growing up in Los Angeles as the daughter of immigrant parents, I witnessed both the quiet sacrifices that sustain families and the structural barriers that often go unseen by others. Early mornings in my father’s car, long afternoons waiting after school, and weekends spent alongside my parents at work taught me discipline, patience, and deep respect for resilience.

As I grew older, the disparities in my community became impossible to ignore—from unhoused neighbors at freeway exits to families navigating systems not built for them. Rather than allowing these realities to become background noise, I began transforming observation into action through academic focus and community work, including the expansion of Project Mosaic.

Today, I carry forward the lessons my parents modeled: that dignity should never depend on circumstance, and that meaningful change requires both technical knowledge and sustained advocacy. My work reflects a long-term commitment to advancing equitable access, protecting innovation responsibly, and ensuring that the communities that shaped me are never overlooked.

Current Priorities & Focus Areas

  • Expanding research experience, particularly through publication opportunities such as my ongoing Lumiere independent research project on housing instability and immigration.
  • Building technical skills in electrical engineering, software development, and data analysis to strengthen my foundation for a future career in patent law.

  • Growing Project Mosaic to create direct impact for marginalized communities while mentoring youth volunteers and expanding community partnerships.
  • Developing leadership, public speaking, and advocacy skills to translate passion into measurable change.
  • Exploring intersections of technology, law, and psychology to address systemic inequities and promote ethical innovation.

Through the Adila Mehjebin’s mentorship during the Lumiere Independent Research Program—where I participated on a full need-based scholarship—I investigated how structural vulnerabilities shape housing insecurity among Guatemalan deportees in their first year after return, uncovering the cyclical forces that sustain instability (explored further in my Projects tab). Through her support, I also connected with Timothy Pham, whose journey from electrical engineering into an MBA further reinforced my intended path. Together, these mentors exemplify the kind of guidance, authenticity, and encouragement that continues to shape both my ambitions and my commitment to pay mentorship forward.
